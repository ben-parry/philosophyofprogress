An Introduction to the Origins of Progress

Spring 2025 | Benjamin Parry | ben@benparry.ca | _benjaminparry

"The world revolves around the inventors of new values, it revolves invisibly. But the people and fame revolve around actors… Far from the marketplace and from fame happens all that is great, far from the marketplace and from fame the inventors of new values have always dwelt…"
- Nietzsche
"All our progress is an unfolding, like the vegetable bud. You have first an instinct, then an opinion, then a knowledge, as the plant has root, bud, and fruit. Trust the instinct to the end, though you can render no reason. It is vain to hurry it. By trusting it to the end it shall ripen into truth, and you shall know why you believe."
- Ralph Waldo Emerson
"For since the law has but a shadow of the good things to come instead of the true form of these realities, it can never, by the same sacrifices that are continually offered every year, make perfect those who draw near."
- Hebrews 10:1

Over the last several centuries our world has experienced profound progress. The first flush toilet was introduced in the 1790s. In 1973 an astronaut took a shower onboard an orbital space station. In 1865 men and women were sold into slavery in the US. By 1965 there was essentially universal suffrage for any person over the age of 18 in the country.

We have no science of progress, we don't even have clear definitions, so we have no way to say for sure, but today there is a question about whether this rate of change is slowing. Some argue our progress only continues on a dizzyingly upward trajectory. Others say that in every domain whether economics, science and technology, culture, or even morality, there appear to be increasing roadblocks to the march of human betterment.

We don't know what led to our progress and whether, or why, it is now slowing. Yet, there are few more pressing questions. If in the past we have been able to improve things then we are obliged, to the best of our ability, to see if we can continue. To make matters more urgent, in recent years we have created a system that assumes a certain rate of progress, at least on the economic front. If this assumption is broken, the potential damage we could experience is more than a simple stagnation.

The aim of this seminar1 is to investigate the philosophical and theological foundations of progress.

First we will look at the genealogy of progress: Where did our ideas about progress come from? This starts with the oldest, religious and philosophical, ideas about progress, What are they? What are they doing? Are they an obstacle to progress or intimately related to it? Do they matter at all?

We will then look at the new, now dominant, secular and scientific worldview that developed in the European Enlightenment and try to get a sense through the contrast with past ideas of what actually changed in our way of approaching the world.

Then in the second half we will try to develop our own beliefs of what progress is and investigate which ideas might hold promise for future progress: What was the nature of our recent progress? Did it in fact depend on ideas at all or were the ideas tangential? How did our worldviews change? Was it really progress? What's next?

If you're taking this course there is a good chance you are more interested in participating in progress than simply studying it. Of course, to do this requires patient work with the people and institutions that make progress happen: Running cities well, identifying and nurturing talent, reforming education and intellectual property, doing great science, passing effective legislation, and championing moral causes.

The study of what enables success in these areas is important but it is best addressed in later courses. For now we're just getting our bearings. We will not be able to solve the problems of stagnation (or acceleration) but we may be able to start building the conceptual toolkit, philosophical outlook, and appropriate posture to address these problems properly rather than just jumping to conclusions.

A note on cross-cultural analysis

Throughout the seminar we are looking primarily at 'Western' texts. This has to be the case. The 'Western' story is intimately tied to any understanding of the progress of the last few hundred years. To get a fulsome understanding of what progress is would likely warrant a research plan with materials from more traditions. But, we have constraints on time.

When not reviewing 'Western texts,' Chinese works have been selected as the other source for consideration. This allows us to gain some degree of depth in one area. It may also be a welcome addition to help us understand what it means for the idea of progress as a 'Western' phenomenon in a world where a modern Chinese state plays an increasingly dominant role.

Week 1: Why Study Progress?

Why bother studying progress at all? Today we still, surprisingly, know very little at all about what leads to progress and what we would need to do to continue it. Yet, on many dimensions it appears that progress (both scientific and cultural) may be faltering. It is not clear if this is a scientific or spiritual issue but figuring out what is going on is an urgent task.

Week 2: An Introduction to Religious Ideas about Progress

We'll start by looking at some of our earliest ideas about progress. How did we imagine a better world in our earliest religious ideation? And how did that develop over time.

"Religion" is about as amorphous a category as could possibly be proposed and the potential scope of religious ideas is vast. To keep our study concrete we'll look at three 'ideas' across three traditions. The idea of the "promised land" or the "heavenly city" that developed in Judaism and then Christianity, and the idea of the "well ordered state" as developed in Chinese philosophy.

Key Questions to Consider

Week 3: An Introduction to Secular Ideas about Progress

Starting in Europe, by the time of Descartes, we began to enter 'modernity' and our ideas about the world and what constituted progress experienced a profound shift. This new ecosystem was deeply concerned with the correct relations between men, the primacy of rationality, and a distrust of received institutions.

Key Questions to Consider

What ideas formed the backdrop for the progress that happened? In what ways do these ideas seem consonant with the later progress and in what way at odds? In what way are these still our society's beliefs today? In what way are they different? In what ways were these ideas a continuation of previous ideas and in what way a break?

Week 4: An Introduction to Scientific Ideas

One of our background beliefs about progress is that it has been spurred on as the result of scientific ideas. But what exactly are scientific 'ideas'? This week we are going to look at some of the most important scientific ideas from the beginning of the scientific revolution until today.

Note that one of the unique elements of scientific ideas is that their narrative structures are brief compared to the other kinds of philosophical and historical works we are considering in this course. This week therefore has many more short passages to consider, with some brief overviews by key texts on the scientific method.

Key Questions to Consider

Is there something unique in the kind of reasoning employed in science vs. other reasoning? What about compared to religious reasoning? If so, in what way is that related to progress? What is a science of progress? How has scientific reasoning changed over time? Is this progress?

Week 5: The Edge of Science

One of the causes of our lack of progress today — at least on a technological front — appears to be a relative stagnation within the scientific community itself. After the feats of the 1800s and early 1900s our breakthroughs are now relatively rare (at the very least when compared to the manpower required). Part of the reason may be that our scientific paradigm blinds us to alternative avenues of scientific progress. Where is our paradigm weak and what could we do about it?

The potential blindspots for our current paradigm are endless. However, the field of Complexity Science offers many concrete pointers for some of the issues we face from within the currently dominant form of scientific reasoning. By studying some of its key ideas we may be able to uncover paths forward.

Week 6: What Happened? Economics, Culture and the Enchantment with Technology

Going into this second half of the course the most important issue that confronts us is the one formulated by Joseph Needham. Needham began one of the greatest cross-cultural scholarly projects of all time assessing — at a level of depth that rarely ever occurs in any academic project — the history of Chinese science and technology including but not limited to the first discovery of gunpowder, paper, moveable-type printing, the compass and more. Doing so led him to a profound mystery.

He formulated this in The Needham Question: "Why did modern science, the mathematization of hypotheses about Nature, with all its implications for advanced technology, take its meteoric rise only in the West at the time of Galileo?", or more pointedly: "why, between the first century BC and the fifteenth century AD, [was] Chinese civilization much more efficient than occidental in applying human natural knowledge to practical human needs"

In this section we will start to look at some ways of answering this question through the lens of economics, technology, culture, and religion.

Week 8: What Happened? Did we actually progress?

The other nagging question is are we sure this was progress? It's hard to argue with the way statistics are presented around what appear to be unassailable goods like life expectancy and literacy. However, when it comes to questions of sovereignty, morals, virtues, and even beauty the answer is not quite so clear. Did we really make progress?

Week 9: What's Next? This cannot last (or can it?)

Following the scientific and technological progress of the last centuries it appears that our world is stagnating. Is this due to fundamental limits to progress or is it simply the end of specific secular growth trends and our job now is to find new ones?

The rapid decline in fertility poses another question. While further scientific and technological progress may be possible it appears that at least so far progress has been correlated with a need for ever more manpower to improve at the edges as well as a decrease in fertility. Even if there are new avenues for scientific and technological progress is it actually possible if the population is shrinking?

There are also the questions raised by an increasingly powerful China: How much of our recent progress depends on American leadership? Is there something unique about the American ability to develop science and technology? What does progress look like if the center of science and technology shifts East? Is that even possible?

Wang Huning is the fourth ranking member of the CCP politburo standing committee, the top decision making body in China, and the chief ideologue for the party. To get a handle on these questions and how they are being played out in reality, there is probably no better work than his study of the U.S. in 美国反对美国 (America Against America).
